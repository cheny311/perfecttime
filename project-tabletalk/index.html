0 — Overview

1 — What happens at the table

English

The table is set like a familiar kitchen scene.

A voice speaks.

On the stove, there is a pan. A basket of ingredients. And several printed recipes for reference.

The recipes give instructions.

They resemble everyday cooking guides, but involve components and processes that are less familiar.

As ingredients are placed into the pan, corresponding voices begin to appear.

Different actions trigger different responses.

Some voices address only the person standing at the table.

Others persist, regardless of where you are in the space.

At certain moments, the recipe invites direct interaction with the system.

Raising your voice may interrupt the process— but only briefly.

It does not ask questions. It does not explain itself. It continues.

Participants may follow the instructions, hesitate, interrupt, or leave actions unfinished.

But none of these choices bring the system to a stop.

2 — How the system works

Table Talk operates through a set of simple, rule-based interactions.



Physical actions occurring around the table trigger changes in sound and visual elements. The system listens, measures, and responds.


Weight trigger: A load cell installed beneath the table detects what ingredients are placed into the pan.

Different weight ranges correspond to different audio and visual states.

These states activate spoken voices—some comment on whether the ingredient is “appropriate” or “fresh,” while others shift toward evaluating the participant’s behavior.

Sound & interruption

Volume trigger: Beside the table stands an upright ear. A microphone is embedded inside it to monitor the ambient sound level.

When the volume exceeds a certain threshold, the system pauses. Temporarily.

After few seconds, the system resumes—often responding by commenting on the participant’s reaction, suggesting that they were “overreacting.”

Body interruption: The installation includes a soft sculpture in the form of an enlarged human face. When participants place their hands into the nostrils, they can retrieve two smaller faces with embedded pressure sensors. Pressing them interrupts everything: sound and visuals stop at once.

This interruption is achieved through bodily action—no speech required. After a delay, the system returns to its default state, sometimes warning the next participant: “Don’t be like her.” It doesn’t remember people; each action is processed in real time as an isolated event.

3 — Moving through the space

The room is divided into three zones.

They are not arranged to guide you gently through the space. They face each other.

Projection Zone

At one end of the room, a close-up video of a man’s mouth is projected onto the wall. It speaks continuously.

The voice delivers instructions, advice, corrections— the kind that sound reasonable, familiar, even caring.

You don’t activate it. It’s already there when you arrive.

Only the person standing directly in front of the projection hears the scolding clearly. Others hear mumbled speech.

The voice feels directional. It knows who it is speaking to.

Technically: MetaHuman (UE5) lip sync + AI voice, with a green-painted face as a symbol of replaceability.

Cooking Zone

Directly facing the projection is a kitchen table.

Ingredients placed into the pan trigger different voices and visuals. A microphone can pause the system—briefly.

After the pause, it resumes and comments on your reaction.

Face Interaction Zone

In a corner, slightly away from the main axis, there is a large soft sculpture of a human face.

Inside the nostrils are two smaller faces embedded with pressure sensors. Pressing them stops everything at once.

After a delay, the system returns. When the next person approaches the table, the voice begins again.
